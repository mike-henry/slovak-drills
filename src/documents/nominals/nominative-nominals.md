# Nominative Case

Base dictionary form.  
Used for subjects and predicate nouns.

---

## 1. Noun Stems

### Masculine

- Consonant-ending kept: _hrad → hrad_
- -a → remove **a**: _kolega → koleg-_
- -o/-e remove vowel
- -ec animate → drop **e**: _chlapec → chlapc-_

### Feminine

- -a → remove **a**
- -ia → remove **ia**
- consonant-ending unchanged

### Neuter

- -o/-e → remove vowel
- -ie → remove **ie** → **i**
- -um → remove **um**

</section>

## 2. Nouns — Nominative Singular

### Masculine

- base form: _chlap, muž, hrdina, stroj_

### Feminine

- base form: _žena, kniha, kosť_

### Neuter

- base form: _mesto, auto, srdce_

</section>

<section id="nouns-plural">

## 3. Nouns — Nominative Plural

### Masculine

- animate → **-i**: _chlapi, muži_
- masc ending -a → **-ovia**: _hrdinovia, kolegovia_
- inanimate hard → **-y**: _hrady, plány_
- inanimate soft → **-e**: _stroje_

### Feminine

- -a → **-y**
- -ia → **-ie**
- consonant-ending → **-i**

### Neuter

- -o → **-á**
- -e → **-ia**
- -ie → **-ia**
- -um → **-á**

</section>

### Pronouns — nominative

Nominative pronouns are used **as subjects of verbs** or **predicate nouns**.  
They indicate **who is performing the action** and agree in **person and number**.  
They **never appear after a governing preposition** (except in rare cases with linking prepositions for emphasis).

---

</section>
<section id="pronouns-short-forms">

#### **Nominative Pronouns — Short Forms (unstressed)**

Short forms are **neutral, unstressed forms**, typically used in everyday speech.  
They appear **directly in subject position**.

**Key points:**

- Neutral and casual
- Immediately serve as subject
- Rarely used in predicate position

**Examples:**

- _Ja som doma._ — I am at home
- _Ty vieš pravdu._ — You know the truth
- _On ide do školy._ — He goes to school

**Notes:**  
Short forms = unstressed, used as subject in neutral contexts.

</section>

<section id="pronouns-long-forms">

#### **Nominative Pronouns — Long Forms (stressed/emphatic)**

Long forms are **stressed or emphatic**, used to **highlight the subject** or contrast it.  
They may appear **later in the sentence** for emphasis or formal style.

**Key points:**

- Emphasizes the subject
- Flexible placement
- Often used in contrast or formal writing

**Examples:**

- _Ja, a nie on, pôjdem._ — **I**, not him, will go
- _Ty, nie ty, máš pravdu._ — **You**, not someone else, are correct

**Notes:**  
Long forms = stressed/emphatic, may appear in predicate or contrastive position.

</section>

<section id="pronouns-after-prep">

#### **Nominative Pronouns — After Preposition Forms**

Generally, nominative pronouns **do not appear after prepositions**, as the nominative case is not governed by prepositions.

**Usage Notes:**

- Prepositions normally require **accusative, dative, locative, or instrumental**
- Only in archaic or fixed expressions might nominative appear after prepositions (_okolo **ja**, not standard_)

**Examples:**

- None standard — nominative pronouns do **not follow prepositions**.

</section>

<section id="pronouns-sentence-order">

#### **Nominative Pronouns — Sentence Order and Usage Notes**

**Key points:**

- Always act as subject of the sentence
- Short forms = neutral subject
- Long forms = emphasize or contrast
- Predicative use (after _byť_, _stať sa_) often uses long forms for emphasis

**Examples:**

- _Ja som učiteľ._ — neutral, short form
- _Ja som ten, kto učí._ — emphatic, long form
- _On je lekár._ — subject pronoun in neutral context

**Summary:**

- Short → unstressed, neutral subject
- Long → stressed/emphatic, contrastive or formal
- After-prep → not applicable in nominative

</section>
